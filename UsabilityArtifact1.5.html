Technology and Social Justice

This philosophy is a respons to a discussion question from the Usability of Systems and Services course.

My Philosophy on Technology & Social Justice

In some cases, technology creates a greater divide between the haves and the have-nots. For example, during COVID-19 and distance learning, students who lack access to technology may fall further behind their peers in school. Technology also has the potential to help make school and work more equitable for those with disabilities, recent immigrants, and students in poor or remote locations. An easy-to use text to speech app could help someone who is blind to navigate a web page. The same could be true for a translation app that could be used by a recent immigrant.

Usability can also benefit students in remote or underfunded schools. Technology could give them access to AP courses not normally offered in their school. A college-finder app could help them to learn about schools and financial aid information that would be previously unknown to them. These tools could help make students more competitive with their peers for college admissions and future job prospects.

In order to level the playing field for all, the technology must be easily available to all types of users. Once the technology is available, it must be usable enough to help the users easily navigate the world around them.
